1. CHALLENGE OVERVIEW & CORE OBJECTIVE 
This challenge asks you to build a complete, automated Factory Compliance and Alert Escalation System. 
The system must bridge three domains that are rarely integrated in a single pipeline: computer vision 
(detecting what is happening in a video), natural language understanding (extracting compliance rules from 
an unstructured regulatory document), and operational workflow automation (routing events to the correct 
alert or logging channel based on risk severity). 
 
There is no prescribed architecture. You are free to choose your own frameworks, models, and design 
patterns. The system will be evaluated as a complete functional system.  
 
CORE 
OBJECTIVE 
Ingest raw factory video feeds. Parse a formal EHS compliance policy document. Detect 
behavioral violations in the video against the policy rules. Classify each violation by 
severity. Route alerts and logs accordingly. Present all of this through a live op erations 
dashboard. 
 
1.1  Architectural Freedom 
You may implement this system using any combination of technologies. Reasonable choices might include 
(but are in no way limited to): 
• Vision models: fine-tuned object detectors, zero-shot vision-language models, or classical computer 
vision techniques. 
• Policy parsing: LLM-based rule extraction, retrieval-augmented generation (RAG), structured prompt 
engineering, or manual rule transcription backed by a structured schema.  
• Backend: Python (FastAPI, Flask, Django), Node.js, or any framework that supports a real -time event 
pipeline. 
• Frontend / Dashboard: React, Vue, Streamlit, Gradio, Dash, or any functional web interface.  
• Storage: SQLite, PostgreSQL, MongoDB, or any persistent store appropriate to the data volume.

2. PROVIDED INPUTS 
2.1  Raw Video Clips 
You will receive a collection of Full HD (1920×1080) video clips captured from fixed -angle security cameras 
installed in an active manufacturing facility. The clips have the following characteristics:  
 
Property Value 
Resolution 1920 × 1080 (Full HD) 
Clip Duration 3 to 18 seconds per clip 
Camera Type Fixed-angle IP security cameras 
Environment Indoor manufacturing facility — active production floor 
Content Ambient shop-floor traffic, forklift movements, human-machine interactions, 
pedestrian routing, and stationary equipment states 
 
2.2  Compliance Policy Document 
You will also receive the facility's Occupational Health and Safety Compliance Policy Manual as a PDF. 
This is the authoritative source of truth for all compliance decisions your system makes. Key characteristics 
of this document: 
 
• It defines the facility's behavioral compliance framework across four operational domains: pedestrian 
movement, equipment intervention, electrical panel management, and forklift load management.  
• For each domain, it specifies both the required (safe) behavior and the prohibited (unsafe) behavior, 
along with the observable indicators that distinguish them. 
• It describes the automated monitoring and alerting infrastructure installed at the facility, including the 
types of alerts triggered upon detection and the reporting format used.  
 
Your system must parse this document to extract the compliance rules that drive your detection and 
severity classification logic.  
3. FUNCTIONAL MODULES 
Your system must implement all five of the following modules. Each module has defined inputs, outputs, 
and interface contracts with the other modules. The modules must function as an integrated pipeline , 
outputs from one feed into the next. 
 
Module 1 — Detection Engine 
ROLE Ingest video clips and identify behavioral compliance violations by cross-referencing observed 
activity against the rules extracted from the compliance policy document.  
 
Required Behavior 
• Accept raw video clips as input. 
• Process each clip frame-by-frame or as a temporal sequence as required by your detection approach. 
• Identify and localize any behavioral violations belonging to the compliance categories defined in the 
policy document.

Policy Grounding Requirement 
The detection logic must be grounded in the compliance policy document. Specifically:

NOTE

set. Document your model selection rationale and any known limitations in your repository 
README.

Module 5 — Operations Dashboard 
ROLE Provide a functional GUI or web application for facility overseers to monitor compliance status, 
review alerts, and export audit records. 
 
Required Dashboard Features 
The dashboard must implement all three of the following functional views: 
 
View A — Live Feed Monitor 
• Display a live or simulated feed of processed video clips with compliance status indicators overlaid or 
adjacent to the clip view. 
• Status indicators must reflect the current detection state: no violation detected, violation detected 
(with severity tier color coding), or alert active. 
• For High and Critical events, the dashboard must render the real-time alert triggered by Module 3 
(e.g., a visual flash, banner, or modal notification). 
 
View B — Alert Timeline Stream 
• Display a real-time or near-real-time chronological stream of compliance events as they are detected. 
View C — Historical Log & Export 
• Provide access to the full historical compliance log persisted by Module 4.  
• Allow the user to filter records by date range, severity tier, and behavior class.  
• Provide a functional export button that downloads the filtered or full log in at least one of the report 
formats specified in Module 4 (JSON, CSV, or database dump). 
 
NOTE The dashboard does not need to be production-polished in terms of visual design. It must, 
however, be functional.

5. HINTS & POINTS TO THINK ABOUT 
The following are not instructions — they are ideas to help you think through the harder parts of the 
problem. You may find some useful, others irrelevant depending on your approach.  
 
On Policy Parsing 
• The compliance policy defines each unsafe behavior in terms of an observable visual indicator (e.g., a 
vest color, a block count, a panel state, a spatial boundary). What is the cleanest way to extract these 
indicator-to-class mappings from prose text? 
• The policy's hazard context sections, warning callouts, and frequency data are signals about severity. 
How does the language differ between a section describing a 'WARNING' condition and one 
describing a 'CRITICAL SAFETY NOTICE'? 
• If you use an LLM to parse the policy, how will you verify that its extracted rules are faithful to the 
source document? What could go wrong, and how would you catch it? 
 
On Detection 
• Some of the four compliance classes are easier to detect from video than others. Consider which are 
state-based (a condition that persists across many frames) versus action-based (a brief behavioral 
event). Does this affect your choice of model or approach? 
• The policy describes observable indicators for each behavior. How close is your vision model's 
internal representation to those human-readable indicators? Does it need to be? 
• If your detector is uncertain about a borderline case (e.g., a forklift carrying what might be two or three 
blocks), how should your system handle that? Does the policy give you any guidance?  
 
On Severity and Escalation 
• Two of the four behavior categories appear in the compliance policy under a 'WARNING' callout and 
two appear under a 'CRITICAL SAFETY NOTICE' callout.  
• The escalation pipeline's routing rules are binary (Low/Medium → log; High/Critical → alert). What 
happens if you detect multiple simultaneous violations of different severities in the same clip? How 
should the pipeline handle that? 
 
On the Dashboard 
• The compliance policy document describes the real deployed system's dashboard interface , and what 
it displays (live camera feed, text log of events). This may inform your dashboard design.  
• The alert timeline stream needs to be filterable. Consider how your data model supports filtering 
efficiently.
